### Problem 1: Ekari Numerals

Let's analyze the pattern in the given Ekari numerals:

- wija = 2
- rati = 5
- joka = 3
- mepija = 4
- beo = 5
- daemita = 10
- moeto = 60
- enama = 3
- ka = and
- ma = and (used with larger numbers)

The number system appears to be base-5 and base-60.

(a) Writing in digits:

- moeto waroewo = 60 × 4 = 240
- ijema joka rati = 20 + 3 × 5 = 35
- moeto wido ma benomi ka wi = 60 × 5 + 16 + 1 = 317

The numeral that has the same value as one above is "ijema joka rati" (35), which is the same as "ije mepija" (39) minus 4.

(b) Writing out in Ekari:

1. wi
19. ijema mepija
26. ijema joka rati ka wi
104. moeto wi ma ije ka mepija
292. moeto mepija ma ije ka wido ka wija

The number that can be expressed in two ways is 1:
- wi
- moeto wi ma ije ka wido ka wija (60 × 0 + 20 + 15 + 2 - 36 = 1)

### Problem 3: Kilivila Sentences

Analyzing the patterns in the given sentences:

- Noun classifiers: te- (human male), na- (human female), ma- (non-human), kwe- (thing)
- Number markers: -tala (one), -yu (two), -vasi (four), -vila (how many)
- Tense markers: le- (past), bi- (future)
- Word order: VSO (Verb-Subject-Object)

(a) Translating into English:

14. Navila vivila biyamata tomwaya mtona?
    "How many women will look after this old man?"

15. Bikamkwamsi kweyu vivila minasina.
    "Those women will eat two things."

16. Amagudina gwadi lekota?
    "Which child arrived?"

17. Tevila tauwau bigisesi gugwadi gudigasisi?
    "How many men will see the wild children?"

18. Legisesi ketala waga vivila minasiwena.
    "Those women saw one canoe." OR "One canoe saw those women."
    (This sentence has two possible translations due to ambiguous word order)

(b) Translating into Kilivila:

19. How many canoes did those old women see?
    Navila waga legisesi nunumwaya minasiwena?

20. These four white men will look after this clever child.

### Problem 4: Agbirigba Secret Language

The pattern for forming Agbirigba words from Ogbakiri words:

1. Insert 'tV' before each syllable, where 'V' is a copy of the following vowel.
2. If the syllable starts with a vowel, use 'tɪ' for front vowels (i, e, ɛ) and 'tʊ' for back vowels (u, o, ɔ, a).
3. For nasal vowels, use 'tṼ' where 'Ṽ' is a copy of the following nasal vowel.

(a) The exception is "òtṹtṹ" (to look for). Its expected form would be "òtʊ́tṹ".

(b) Agbirigba forms of the given Ogbakiri words:

1. rʊ́tʊ̂ (chewing-stick) → tʊ́rʊ́tʊ̀tʊ̂

### Problem 5: Rikbaktsa Verbs

Analyzing the patterns in Rikbaktsa verbs:

- Prefixes: a- (1st person object), tsi- (2nd person subject), ni- (3rd person subject)
- Suffixes: -naha (1st person plural subject)
- Tense: Present/past tense has no specific marker, future tense uses my- or pi-

(a) Translating into English:

- aharapyryk: "I hugged you_{pl}" / "he hugged you_{pl}"
- mymyboro: "I will bite you_{pl}" / "he will bite you_{pl}"
- uru: "you_{sg} slept"
- tsiksiwynaha: "we cut you_{sg} up"
- ikwak: "I hunted" / "he hunted"
- ziksi: "you_{pl} left"

(b) Translating into Rikbaktsa:

- we will fall: tsimyknaranaha
- I bathed: ikmoro
- you_{sg} bit us: tsikboronaha
- I will wait for them / he will wait for them: mysipery
- I will hug you_{pl} / he will hug you_{pl}: myharapyryk
- you_{sg} will leave: tsimyksi
- I killed it: ibeze

These translations demonstrate the complex verb structure in Rikbaktsa, including person and number marking, tense indicators, and object incorporation.
