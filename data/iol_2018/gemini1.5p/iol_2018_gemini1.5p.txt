**(b) Some Terena words are borrowed from Portuguese…**

1. **In what way are these words different from native Terena words?**

These words are different from native Terena words because they don't follow the regular pattern of first-person and second-person singular forms. In native words, the second person form usually replaces the initial "n" sound with a "x" sound. However, in these borrowed words,  the second person form replaces the initial "l" sound with a "le" sound.

2. **Translate into Terêna: my tin can, my cloak.**

* My tin can: **látaña**
* My cloak: **kápaña** 


## Problem 4 Solution

**(a)**

The word X is "**atuh**", which means "**and**". 

The linguist thought it was missing in these examples:

* **Example 2:** nybat biabys juhurubys (6 eggs) - It should be "nybat **atuh** biabys juhurubys".
* **Example 8:** wiwis biabys bøbys (14 betel nuts) - It should be "wiwis **atuh** biabys bøbys".
* **Example 11:** anowip biabys juhurubys (26 eggs) - It should be "anowip **atuh** biabys juhurubys".

**How it is implied by the context:**

The word "atuh" is implied because the numbers 6, 14, and 26 are all formed by adding a set of 10 to the base numbers (6, 4, and 16 respectively).  The use of "atuh" isn't mandatory when expressing these compound numbers, as the context makes it clear that addition is being used.

**(b) Translate into English:**

12. biabys atub bøbys - **7 betel nuts**
13. atub juhurub - **4 eggs**
14. nybat atuh araruh - **5 sugar canes**
15. biagas atugas biagas baigas - **12 sheets of sago bark**
16. wiwis baigas - **13 sheets of sago bark**

**(c) Translate into Mountain Arapesh:**
